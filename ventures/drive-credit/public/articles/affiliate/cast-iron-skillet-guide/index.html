Choose the Right Skillet for Your Needs

You should start with a 10-inch or 12-inch skillet. This size offers the most versatility for a single person or a small family. A 10-inch skillet (like the Lodge L8SK3) is perfect for searing a single steak, frying two eggs, or baking a small cornbread. It’s manageable for most stovetops and ovens. If you regularly cook for 3-4 people, or envision making larger batches of food, a 12-inch skillet (such as the Lodge L10SK3) provides ample surface area. Larger skillets, up to 15 inches, are great for whole chickens or big family meals, but they are heavy and take up significant storage space. Smaller skillets, 6-8 inches, are specialized for single servings or side dishes, and less useful as your primary pan. For your first cast iron, prioritize that 10 or 12-inch workhorse. You’ll find new cast iron skillets from reputable brands like Lodge, Victoria, or Smithey Ironware Company ranging from $20 for a basic Lodge 10-inch to $200+ for a premium Smithey. Don't feel pressured to buy the most expensive. A Lodge will serve you beautifully for a lifetime.

Season Your Skillet Properly, Then Maintain It

You need to season your cast iron skillet to create its non-stick surface and protect it from rust. Most new cast iron, especially Lodge, comes "pre-seasoned." This means it has a thin layer of polymerized oil baked on at the factory. This initial seasoning is a good start, but it's not perfect. You will build upon it. To further season your skillet, first wash it with warm water and a tiny bit of dish soap (yes, dish soap is fine for modern cast iron, just don't let it soak). Dry it immediately and thoroughly with a towel. Place it on a burner over low heat for 5 minutes to ensure all moisture evaporates. Next, apply a very thin layer of high smoke point oil – grapeseed, flaxseed, or even vegetable oil – to the entire skillet, inside and out. Use a paper towel to wipe off any excess, as if you’re trying to remove it all. You just want a microscopic film. Place the skillet upside down in an oven preheated to 450-500°F (232-260°C) for one hour. Place aluminum foil on the rack below to catch any drips. Turn off the oven and let the skillet cool completely inside. Repeat this process 2-3 times for a robust initial seasoning. After each use, you’ll clean it, dry it, and apply a thin coat of oil, which builds the seasoning further. This consistent maintenance is key.

Cook a Wide Range of Foods with Confidence

You can cook almost anything in cast iron. It excels at high-heat searing, achieving a crust on steaks, chicken, and fish that other pans struggle to replicate. Try a reverse-seared steak: bake it in the oven to your desired internal temperature, then transfer it to a screaming hot cast iron skillet on the stovetop for a minute or two per side. You’ll get an incredible crust. It’s also fantastic for frying. Crispy fried chicken, perfectly golden hash browns, and even donuts are all within its wheelhouse. Don't forget baking. Cornbread, biscuits, deep-dish pizza, and even fruit crisps turn out beautifully with a wonderfully browned crust thanks to the even heat distribution. You can start dishes on the stovetop and finish them in the oven without transferring. Think frittatas, shakshuka, or roasted vegetables. The only things you should avoid, especially with new seasoning, are highly acidic foods like tomato sauces or deglazing with wine for extended periods. These can strip the seasoning. As your seasoning builds up over months and years, you can get away with more, but it’s best to be cautious initially.

Clean and Store Your Skillet for Longevity

You must clean your cast iron skillet immediately after use to prevent food from sticking and to maintain its seasoning. While it’s still warm (but not scorching hot), scrape out any large food bits with a spatula. For stuck-on food, use hot water and a stiff brush or a chainmail scrubber (like The Ringer). If necessary, use a tiny amount of mild dish soap, but avoid abrasive pads like steel wool. Rinse thoroughly. The most crucial step is drying. Place the skillet back on a burner over low heat for 5-10 minutes until all water has evaporated. You can even see steam rising. If you skip this, it will rust. Once dry, and while it’s still warm, apply a very thin layer of cooking oil (vegetable, grapeseed, or even canola) to the entire surface, inside and out, with a paper towel. Wipe off any excess. It should look dry, not greasy. Store your skillet in a dry place. If stacking, place a paper towel or cloth between skillets to prevent scratching and allow air circulation. Never put it in the dishwasher. Never let it air dry. Never leave it soaking in the sink.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

You will make mistakes, but knowing them upfront can save you frustration. The biggest mistake is not drying it thoroughly enough. Rust is the enemy of cast iron, and moisture is its best friend. Always heat it dry after washing. Another common error is using too much oil when seasoning or re-oiling. A thick layer of oil will become sticky and gummy. You want a microscopically thin film. Wipe it off until you think you’ve removed too much; that’s usually just right. Cooking acidic foods too soon is another pitfall. Tomatoes, lemons, and vinegar can strip the fresh seasoning. Wait until your skillet has a robust, well-established seasoning (typically after 6-12 months of regular use and re-oiling) before experimenting with these ingredients. Lastly, being afraid to use it is a mistake. Some people baby their cast iron too much. The more you cook with it, the better the seasoning becomes. Don't be scared of high heat or everyday use. Cast iron is designed to be a workhorse.

Q

Can I really use soap on cast iron?

A

Yes, you can. Modern dish soaps are much milder than the lye-based soaps of old that would strip seasoning. A small amount of mild dish soap will not harm a properly seasoned cast iron skillet. The key is to rinse thoroughly and dry immediately and completely.

Q

What if my cast iron skillet rusts?

A

Don't panic! Rust is fixable. Scour the rusted area thoroughly with steel wool or a stiff brush until all the rust is gone. Wash, dry completely on the stove, and then re-season the affected area (or the entire pan) several times following the seasoning instructions above.

Q

How often do I need to re-season my skillet?

A

You don't need to do a full oven re-seasoning often. If you clean, dry, and apply a thin coat of oil after every use, your seasoning will naturally build and maintain itself. A full oven re-seasoning is usually only necessary if you've stripped the seasoning (e.g., from cooking acidic foods for too long) or if rust appears.
