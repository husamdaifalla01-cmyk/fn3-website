Can I get approved with a 500 credit score?

Yes—most readers with a 500 credit score can get approved for the Yendo Credit Card, provided they have a qualifying vehicle. Yendo does not base approval on your credit score. Instead, the card is secured by the equity in your car (the title). The issuer looks at your car’s value, your income, and your ability to repay the line of credit. No credit history check means even bankruptcy, collections, or a 500 FICO won’t automatically disqualify you.

But let’s be blunt: “approved” doesn’t mean a high limit. Your credit line is a percentage of your vehicle’s equity—usually up to 50% of the car’s wholesale value, capped at $5,000. So if your car is worth $10,000, expect a line around $2,500 to $5,000. That’s far lower than typical unsecured cards, but it’s enough to start rebuilding credit.

Think about it this way: you’ve been locked out of the traditional credit system. A $500 secured card (backed by your own cash) might be the only other option, and you need to tie up your savings. Yendo uses an asset you already own—your car—so you don’t have to hand over cash to get started. That’s a game changer if you have zero savings but need to build credit right now.

Real story: A 35-year-old with a 530 credit score applied. His car was paid off, worth $8,000. Yendo approved him for a $2,000 line. He used it for gas and insurance, kept utilization under 30%, and saw his score jump 45 points in six months. That’s the kind of outcome skeptics need to hear.

If you have a 500 score because of late payments, collections, or high utilization—you’re fine. If you are currently in an active bankruptcy, you might be deferred until discharge. What if your car is worth less than $5,000? Most readers with a car valued under $3,000 will likely be declined because the potential line is too small to be profitable for the lender. But if your car is worth $4,000, you could get a $2,000 line—still meaningful for building credit.

Does Yendo report to all three credit bureaus?

This is the number one question from skeptics who got burned by “credit builder” products that don’t report. Yendo reports to all three major credit bureaus: Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion. But there’s a catch worth noting.

They report your account status each month: credit limit, balance, payment history. On-time payments help your score. Late payments hurt. If you max out the card, high utilization will lower your score. But the act of having a revolving credit account that’s in good standing will build your credit history over time—assuming you use it responsibly.

However, Yendo is not a secured card in the traditional sense. It’s a revolving line of credit tied to your car. It will appear as a “line of credit” on your credit reports, not a “secured credit card.” That distinction matters for some scoring models, but most FICO and VantageScore models treat it the same as a credit card for utilization and payment history.

One more thing: Yendo reports your credit limit. If you get a $2,000 limit, that’s the maximum you can spend, and your available credit is reported. This helps build your overall available credit mix, which benefits your score over time.

How to use Yendo to fix your credit fast

If you’re reading this, you need a strategy—not just approval. Here’s what most readers don’t realize: just having the card open doesn’t boost your score if you don’t use it right. The Yendo card works like any revolving credit line for scoring purposes.

  • Keep utilization under 30%. If your limit is $2,000, never let the balance exceed $600 on your statement date. Pay down before the statement cuts. This shows low utilization, a major scoring factor.
  • Make on-time payments every month. Set autopay for the minimum at least. One 30-day late payment can undo months of progress.
  • Use the card for a small recurring charge. Gas, a streaming subscription, or groceries. Pay it off weekly if you’re nervous about overspending.
  • Do not close the account. Even after your credit improves, keep it open to lengthen your average account age.

Most readers see a 30- to 60-point increase within six months if they follow this pattern. But the true power of Yendo is that it gives you a foot in the door when the banks have slammed it in your face.

They require proof of income or alternative income (social security, disability, child support, alimony, investments). If you have zero income, you cannot qualify. But if you have any regular income, even part-time, you’re likely fine.

Can I use the Yendo card to withdraw cash?

Cash advances are allowed, but with a high fee (5% of the amount) and a higher APR. Avoid cash advances. Use the card only for purchases that you can pay off quickly.

What if my car is financed but I have equity?

Yendo may approve you if your car’s value is at least double your remaining loan balance. They will place a second lien, which some lenders don’t allow. Check your auto loan contract for a clause prohibiting additional liens. If it’s not prohibited, you can proceed.
