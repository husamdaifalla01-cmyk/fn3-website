Debt consolidation vs debt settlement — which is worse for your credit?

Debt settlement will wreck your credit for years. It sounds good: "Pay 50 cents on the dollar!" But here's what they don't tell you: you stop paying your credit cards for months while you "save up" to settle. Those missed payments go on your credit report. Your score tanks. And even after you settle, the account shows as "settled for less than full balance" — a red flag that future lenders see for up to seven years.

Debt consolidation, when done through a loan, doesn't require you to miss a single payment. You take out the loan, pay off the cards immediately, and then make one fixed payment each month. Your credit takes a small hit from the new loan, but that hit is temporary. Meanwhile, your utilization drops to zero — the single biggest factor in credit scoring — and your score typically recovers within three to six months.

Debt management plans from nonprofit agencies are better than settlement, but they still close your credit card accounts and put you on a tight budget. You'll pay off the debt, but you won't have access to credit during the plan. That can be fine if you're disciplined, but it doesn't help your utilization ratio because the cards are closed.

Bankruptcy isn't even in the same conversation. It stays on your credit for seven to ten years, makes it nearly impossible to rent an apartment or get a car loan, and costs thousands in legal fees.
