Why I Started Testing Products Myself

Most product roundups online are written by people who have never held the product. They aggregate Amazon reviews, pull a few star ratings, and call it editorial content. I know because I used to do it that way too. Then I realized something: the people reading these articles are spending their own money. They deserve better than recycled review text. So I changed how I do things. Every product I recommend on Mintbrooks has gone through a real evaluation process. Sometimes I buy it myself. Sometimes a brand sends a sample. Either way, the process is the same, and the recommendation only goes up if it earns it.

Step 1: I Start With a Clear Use Case

Before I look at a single product, I define what I'm actually testing for. "Best hair oil" is too vague. "Best hair oil for color-treated hair that won't weigh down fine strands under $40" is testable. This framing matters because it determines which products even make the shortlist and what success looks like. If I can't define what winning looks like before I start, I shouldn't be writing the article. Too many recommendation pieces exist to justify a product rather than find the right one. I start from the use case, not the product. This sounds obvious but it's genuinely rare in affiliate content. The incentive structure pushes writers toward whatever product pays the highest commission. I resist that by anchoring every test to a specific reader problem before I ever look at which products are available.

Step 2: I Build a Shortlist From Legitimate Sources

Once I know what I'm testing for, I build a shortlist. My sources in order of how much I trust them: dermatologist or specialist endorsements with citations, Reddit communities where enthusiasts self-report long-term use, verified Amazon reviews filtered for 3-star and 4-star ratings (5-star reviews are often incentivized), and editorial sites with disclosed testing methodologies. I avoid: PR-driven "best of" lists that don't disclose how they chose products, any list where every product is conveniently available through the same affiliate program, and review aggregators that don't differentiate between single-use impressions and long-term testing. My shortlists typically include 6–10 products. I narrow to 3–5 for actual testing based on availability, price range, and how well they match the specific use case I defined in step one.

Step 3: I Use the Product Under Real Conditions

This is the part most affiliate sites skip entirely. Real conditions means: I use the product the way a normal person would use it, not in a controlled environment designed to make it look good. For a hair oil, that means I apply it to my actual hair — not a swatch, not a test strip — and I use it for at least two weeks before forming an opinion. For a sous vide cooker, I cook multiple proteins, including something cheap like chicken thighs and something expensive like a steak, and I pay attention to the cleanup as much as the cooking. For a duvet cover, I wash it the way the average person does: in a regular home washer on a normal cycle, and I see how it looks and feels after washing. I take notes throughout. I don't wait until the end to remember how I felt. If something is annoying to use on day three, that gets recorded even if day ten is fine. Cumulative friction matters to the person buying it.

Step 4: I Test the Failure Conditions

A good product recommendation tells you not just what works but what breaks. I specifically try to stress-test each product against the failure mode most likely for that category. For skincare, that means testing whether the product causes irritation when layered with other common actives, not just when used alone. For kitchen tools, it means testing at maximum capacity, not just a comfortable load. For bedding, it means sleeping hot one night and cold another to understand the temperature range. Most affiliate content never encounters a failure condition because the writer never uses the product long enough or aggressively enough. This is why so many "honest reviews" read as press releases. You can only be honest about what something does wrong if you've actually experienced it doing something wrong.

Step 5: I Separate What I Think From What the Data Says

My personal preference doesn't automatically make something a bad recommendation. I prefer unscented products. That doesn't mean scented products are worse — it means they're not for me. I try to be explicit about this distinction in every article. When I write that a product is "best for X," I mean it performs best for that specific scenario as measured by the criteria I set in step one. Not that I personally like it most. This separation matters because affiliate content has a bias problem: writers tend to recommend things they personally enjoy regardless of whether those things are actually best for the reader's situation. I try to catch this by reviewing my shortlist criteria before I finalize my picks and asking: does this recommendation follow from the test, or from my taste?

What I Won't Recommend

There are categories of products I won't put my name on regardless of commission rates. Products with misleading claims that I can't verify through use or cited research. Products where the only positive reviews are clearly incentivized. Products that are genuinely unsafe — flimsy tools, skincare with known irritants marketed as gentle, cheap electrical items without proper certification. Products in categories where I don't have enough real-use experience to evaluate properly; I'd rather skip the article than recommend based on theory. If a product fits all four of those exclusions and still earns a spot in an article, it's because it passed a genuine test. That's the standard. It's not a high bar in theory. In practice, given the incentives around affiliate marketing, it eliminates more than you'd expect.

How I Handle Products Brands Send Me

Brands send me products to review. I accept them because it lets me test a wider range than I could buy myself. But the rules are the same whether I bought it or received it free. The product goes through the same evaluation. If it fails, I either don't write about it or I write honestly about why it didn't make the cut. I never accept a product on the condition that I'll publish a positive review. I've declined partnerships because the brand asked for approval rights on the final article. That's a red flag. An editorial relationship that requires editorial control isn't editorial — it's advertising. I disclose received products in the article. You'll see "this product was sent to us for review" or similar language. If you don't see that disclosure, I bought it.

Common Questions

Q

How long do you test a product before recommending it?

A

It depends on the category. Skincare gets a minimum of two weeks because skin reaction time varies. Kitchen tools get at least five to ten uses covering different scenarios. Bedding gets one full wash cycle plus at least two weeks of regular use. If a product is seasonal or situational, I note the testing window explicitly in the article.

Q

Do you test every single product in every article yourself?

A

For primary picks and runner-ups, yes. For honorable mentions or "also consider" entries, I rely on verified long-term user reports and cross-reference them with my experience in adjacent products. I'm transparent about which is which. If a product appears in an article without personal testing, the article will say so.

Q

What happens if a product you recommended gets worse after a reformulation?

A

I update the article. This is one reason I still have accounts on the communities I used for sourcing. Reformulation complaints are usually louder than new product enthusiasm on enthusiast forums, so they're detectable. If I miss one and you catch it, email me. I take corrections seriously.
