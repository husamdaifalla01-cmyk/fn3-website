How We Tested

To determine the best meal prep containers, we subjected dozens of popular options to a rigorous testing protocol over several weeks. Our criteria focused on real-world performance, durability, and user experience. Here's how we evaluated each container:

  • Leak-Proofing: We filled each container with colored water and then with a thin soup, sealed it, and shook it vigorously, then left it on its side for 24 hours. Any leaks resulted in a deduction.
  • Durability: We performed drop tests from counter height (approximately 3 feet) onto a tiled floor, both empty and full. We also assessed the resilience of lids and seals after repeated opening and closing.
  • Stain and Odor Resistance: Containers were filled with tomato-based sauces and left for 48 hours, then washed. We checked for residual stains and odors, particularly in plastic containers.
  • Thermal Performance: Glass containers were moved directly from the freezer (-4°F / -20°C) to a preheated oven (350°F / 175°C) or microwave. We also assessed how well food reheated evenly in each container.
  • Stackability and Storage: We evaluated how well containers stacked in a refrigerator and nested in a cabinet, both with and without lids, to assess space efficiency.
  • Ease of Cleaning: All containers were run through a dishwasher cycle, and we also hand-washed them to check for food traps and ease of scrubbing.
  • Aesthetics and Design: We considered the overall look and feel of the containers, how they presented food, and their visual appeal in a fridge or on a desk.
  • Value: We weighed the price against the performance, durability, and features offered.

Our testing involved daily use for actual meal prepping, including storing leftovers, packing lunches, and freezing meals. This comprehensive approach allowed us to identify containers that not only met but exceeded our expectations for performance and design.

FAQ

Are glass meal prep containers better than plastic?

Glass meal prep containers are generally preferred for their durability, non-reactive nature, and resistance to stains and odors. They are also oven-safe (without lids) and maintain their clarity over time. Plastic containers are lighter, less prone to breakage, and often more affordable, but can stain, retain odors, and are not typically oven-safe.

How do I prevent my meal prep containers from leaking?

Look for containers with silicone gaskets or rubber seals in the lid and secure snap-lock or clamp mechanisms. Always ensure the lid is properly aligned and fully sealed before transporting. Avoid overfilling, especially with liquids.

Can I put hot food directly into meal prep containers?

For glass containers, it's generally safe to put hot food in, but allow food to cool slightly before sealing and refrigerating to prevent condensation and bacterial growth. For plastic containers, check the manufacturer's guidelines; some plastics are not designed for very hot food and can leach chemicals.

What sizes of meal prep containers are most useful?

A mix of sizes is most useful. Containers around 3-4 cups (700-950ml) are ideal for main meals. Smaller containers (1-2 cups / 250-500ml) are great for snacks, sides, or dressings. Having both single-compartment and multi-compartment options increases versatility.

How do I clean meal prep containers to avoid odors and stains?

For plastic containers, wash immediately after use to prevent stains. For stubborn stains, a paste of baking soda and water can help. For odors, soak with white vinegar or a baking soda solution. Glass containers are naturally more resistant to both.

Are all meal prep containers microwave safe?

No. While many plastic and glass containers are microwave-safe, always check the manufacturer's instructions. Metal containers are never microwave-safe. For glass containers, remove plastic or metal lids before microwaving.
